Day 0
The Calculator Kid
a dev diary by scar  /  episode 0 of ∞

They said Python is easy. They said AI will teach you. They said just ask ChatGPT and — boom — developer. Today I found out "they" were, with great respect, full of it.

my entire skillset at 9am

I knew how to add numbers. That was the complete portfolio. 2 + 2. That was it. That was the act. Honestly felt like enough — why learn more when the robot does it for you?

Challenge: build a calculator. Two numbers, an operation, spit out the answer. Brain-dead simple. The kind of thing you'd assign a very tired intern on their first day. I was humbled by it within four minutes.

When you write those if statements and watch them actually work, something clicks. Not about the code. About the understanding. The calculator calculated. I understood why. Different thing entirely.

Built that one. Then — without a single hand to hold — built three more. Read StackOverflow threads from 2015 back when people still explained things instead of just pasting solutions. Ancient forums. Sacred texts. Caveman discovers fire. It worked.

1 / 3
the loop incident (embarrassing)

The temperature converter broke. Spent a genuinely humiliating stretch of time figuring out why I was getting one output five times instead of five different outputs.

Turns out: I looped the print instead of the input. Moved one line inside the loop. Brain. Backflip. No AI explanation does that to you. That's just failing, staring, fixing, understanding — in exactly that order, which is apparently the only order that counts.

lists are magic (genuinely)

End of day: I'm using lists. .append(). sum(). Storing data, calculating averages, grading on logic I wrote myself. Two hours ago I didn't know lists existed. Now I'm standing at the edge of a very large thing I don't understand yet, which is exciting in a mildly terrifying way.

Everyone's worried AI kills learning. Maybe it does — for people who use it as a crutch. Doing it the old way — reading, thinking, failing, fixing — that's a different thing. That's the thing that actually sticks.

I didn't let Claude spoon-feed me code today. I asked for direction. I hunted the rest. Like a normal person. Like someone who actually wants to understand.

— scar

the actual realization

Started today knowing how to add numbers. Ended it building things. Small things. Dumb things. Mine.

Turns out the easy way was always just the slow way in disguise — it just feels faster until you hit something it can't shortcut. The hard way only feels hard until it doesn't. And then you actually know something.

That's enough to keep going.
