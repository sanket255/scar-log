Day 15
Dictionaries
Make Sense Now
a dev diary by scar  /  episode 15 of ∞

Dictionaries stopped being magic today. They're just organized data — key-value pairs, like a phonebook that Python can read. I don't know why I found this scary for two weeks. It's a phonebook.

lists vs dictionaries (finally understood)

A list gives you values in order. You access them by position — names[0], names[1], which means you have to remember what number corresponds to what person. Fine for three contacts. Unusable for a hundred.

A dictionary gives you values by name. contacts["Sanket"] just works — no counting, no guessing, no off-by-one errors at 1AM. When you need to connect two related things, dictionaries are the tool. That's the whole rule.

three methods i now know exist
.keys() — just the names. Loop when you want to list people.

.values() — just the numbers. Loop when you only care about the data.

.items() — both, as pairs. Loop when you need the full picture.

Not magic. Three different ways to ask the same dictionary for different things. It was always this simple. I was just never shown the map.
1 / 3
zip() — two lists walk into a function

Had two separate lists: names and numbers. Needed one dictionary. zip() pairs them index by index, like a zipper — first name with first number, second with second, all the way down. Wrap it in dict() and you have a dictionary. Clean, readable, done. Not the only way to build one — but the right tool when you're starting with two lists.

the formatting decision (small, matters)

Could've printed the whole dictionary raw for the "show both" option. Quick. One line. Ugly. Chose to loop through .items() and format each line properly instead. Same data. Readable output. Three extra lines. That's thinking like a developer — choosing how something is presented, not just whether it works. The program could've printed {'Sanket': '1234'} at the user. I chose not to subject anyone to that.

File handling: solid. Dictionaries: solid. Understanding level went from 40% two days ago to 85% today. That's not confidence talking — that's actually being able to explain what every line does without looking anything up. Different feeling. Getting used to it.

the actual realization

Dictionaries are just organized pairs. Lists are just ordered values. The whole trick is knowing which one your problem is actually asking for.

Two more days of foundations. Then I'll actually understand my own code — not just the parts I wrote today, but the parts I wrote two weeks ago.

That's the point of going backwards.
